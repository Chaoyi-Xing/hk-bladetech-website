Blade Material Selection Guide

Blade Material Selection

Choosing the right alloy is a balance between Wear Resistance, Toughness, and Cost. Use this engineering guide to find your match.

Entry Level

Standard Tool Steel

Economical option with high shock absorption but limited edge retention. Best for clean, soft materials.

Wear Life
Toughness
Best For: Soft wood, Dry paper, Biomass.
Pros: Low cost, easy to sharpen onsite.
Balanced

Low Alloy (9CrSi, 6CrW2Si)

Added Chromium and Molybdenum for better wear resistance than carbon steel. The budget-friendly all-rounder.

Wear Life
Toughness
Best For: Hard wood, Rubber, Tire, Cable.
Pros: Resists chipping better than D2.
Most Popular

High Chrome (D2, SKD-11, Cr12MoV)

The industry standard for plastic recycling. Heat treated to HRC 58-60 for extended run times.

Wear Life
Toughness
Best For: PET, PE, PP, Plastic bottles, Crates.
Pros: Excellent edge retention for plastics.
Extreme Duty

Tungsten Carbide

Inlaid carbide tips for extreme abrasive environments. Lifespan is 10x-20x longer than standard steel.

Wear Life
Toughness
Best For: Fiberglass, Carbon Fiber, Metal Powder.
Pros: Maximum uptime, minimal changes.
1

Material Hardness

Abrasive materials (Glass fiber, Reinforced plastics) demand D2 or Carbide.

2

Impact Risk

If metal contamination is possible (Bolts, Nuts), choose tough Alloy Steel to prevent shattering.

3

Environment

Wet grinding requires Stainless Steel. High-heat friction requires HSS.

4

Total Cost

Carbide costs more upfront but reduces downtime in 24/7 high-volume production.
